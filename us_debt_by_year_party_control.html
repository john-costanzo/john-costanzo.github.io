US national debt rose from about $257 billion in 1950 to over $35 trillion in 2024.
Sources: US Treasury / OMB historical debt data. Segment color reflects party holding the presidency; shade reflects congressional alignment.

Notes

  • Dark blue: Democratic president + Democratic House & Senate (e.g. Kennedy/LBJ era, early Clinton, Obama's first two years, Biden 2021–22)
  • Light blue: Democratic president with a split Congress (e.g. Clinton 1995–2000 with a Republican Congress, Obama's last six years)
  • Dark red: Republican president + Republican House & Senate (e.g. Eisenhower 1953–54, most of the George W. Bush years, Trump 2017–18)
  • Pink: Republican president with a split or Democrat-controlled Congress (e.g. Reagan with a Dem-held House, Nixon/Ford years, Trump 2019–20)
  • A few historical patterns stand out: debt grew slowly in real terms through most of the postwar period, began accelerating sharply under Reagan, dipped modestly (as a growth rate) during the Clinton surplus years of the late 1990s, then climbed steeply through the Bush-era wars and tax cuts, the 2008–09 financial crisis response, and the COVID-19 pandemic spending surge.
